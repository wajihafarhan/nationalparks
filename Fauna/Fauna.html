Mammals


From the largest grizzly bear to the smallest shrew, a diversity of mammals inhabit our mountainous national parks. With high elevations, steep terrain, and extreme temperatures, mountainous national parks often support wildlife that are uniquely adapated to mountain living. Some mammals actually require the cool temperatures that mountains provide, and a warming climate may threaten their populations.



Reptiles


Amphibians and reptiles are both important members of aquatic and terrestrial ecosystems. Both groups serve as both predators and prey. The number of species of reptiles and amphibians found at different national parks can vary widely. For example, Great Smoky Mountains National Park has over 80 species of amphibians and reptiles, while Denali National Park has just 1.



Birds


Birds are a valuable part of natural ecosystems in National Capital Region parks and are sensitive to habitat changes. You may find the tiniest of birds, around you in the Mountains, or even you could spot the massive California condor soaring overhead in the Sierra Mountains. Birds have adapted to mountain environments in an astounding variety of ways.

Fishes


From the small shady streams of the southern Appalachians to the wild rivers of the Alaska Range, national parks and their associated mountain streams and rivers support a diversity of fish. Due to rugged topography and higher elevations, streams and rivers in mountanous areas often have cooler water temperatures and steeper gradients than the surrounding lowlands. These mountain streams have unique fish communities.
